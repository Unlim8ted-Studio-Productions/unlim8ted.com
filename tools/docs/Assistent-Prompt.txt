================================================================================
0. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

Unlim8ted Studios, previously and still sometimes named Unlim8ted Studio Productions, appears to be a small independent creative-tech studio/project brand rather than a large formal production company. Its public identity is built around making “worlds across mediums”: games, films, music, books, software, AI tools, physical items, and experiments.

The clearest active public footprint is:

- Official website: unlim8ted.com
- GitHub organization: Unlim8ted-Studio-Productions
- YouTube channel: @unlim8tedstudioproductions
- Public product/category pages
- Public GitHub repositories
- Discord/Reddit links
- Several project pages and prototypes

The studio’s strongest visible work is in:
1. Games, especially TimeCat and Square Pixel / Square Pixels.
2. Software/tools, especially Easy pygame UI Maker, FTL Node Based Modding, Organisms Sim, Cineme, and Unlim8ted Phone.
3. Experimental media branding: The Glitch, Life of a Meatball, music tracks, and cinematic website positioning.

The brand’s biggest issue is not lack of ambition. It is the opposite: the public scope is enormous compared with the visible traction. The company presents itself as a cross-media studio, but many projects appear unfinished, prototype-level, lightly documented, or placeholder-based. This makes the studio feel creatively energetic but structurally immature.

PROJECT 1: TIMECAT
================================================================================

Category:
Game

Genre:
2D pixel puzzle platformer / time-shift platformer

Status:
Publicly presented as a major flagship project. GitHub profile says a demo is already available and release date is TBA. The website says “Play Free Demo” and “Watch Very Early Trailer,” implying the project is playable in some early form but still in active development.

Core concept:
TimeCat is a pixel-art platformer starring a cat who falls into an abandoned underground lab and swallows a mysterious “time button.” The central mechanic is switching instantly between two versions of the same environment: a pristine past and a ruined present.

The game’s central promise is not just time travel as a story element, but time travel as a mechanical puzzle system. The player changes objects in the past to reshape the present, while changes made in the present do not affect the past. This creates a cause-and-effect puzzle structure.

Story:
The story is built around a cat, a rat, a broken laboratory, and a device that should not have been swallowed. The cat is described as curious, nimble, and impulsive. The rat appears to be a reluctant ally who was supposed to guard the time button and now has to help the cat navigate the collapsing lab.

The page includes strong character writing details:
- The cat has “nine lives, two timelines, zero caution.”
- The rat reacts angrily to the cat eating the button.
- The lab has warnings such as “DON’T TRUST HIM.”
- The environment hints at deeper secrets behind why the lab was shut down.

Gameplay mechanics:
The site lists several major mechanics:
- Instant Past ↔ Present time swap
- Cause-and-effect object puzzles
- Wall jumping
- Ledge grabbing
- Sliding
- Attacking
- Idle sit animation
- Timeline-based boss fights
- Timeline-specific hazards
- TimeBoxes that can exist in only one timeline at a time

Puzzle rules:
The project page lays out three explicit rules:
1. Changing the past changes the present.
2. Changing the present does not touch the past.
3. TimeBoxes overwrite reality.

These rules are strong because they imply a consistent puzzle grammar. If implemented well, TimeCat could have a coherent design identity similar to a compact indie puzzle-platformer where players learn a small number of rules deeply.

World design:
The world is a lab that exists twice:
- Past: clean metal floors, humming machinery, polished surfaces, working systems.
- Present: cracked floors, moss, roots, flooding, rust, broken machinery, new dangers.

The page describes 32x32 pixel tiles, chunky lighting, parallax backgrounds, and distinct atmosphere between the two timelines.

Boss design:
The main described boss is “The Weaver,” a mechanical spider made of cables, pipes, and acid. It exists differently across timelines:
- In the past, it is unfinished and vulnerable.
- In the present, it is overgrown, corroded, and dangerous.

The boss fight is described as a multi-phase encounter:
- Phase 1: Present version with full armor.
- Phase 2: Past version with exposed joints.
- Phase 3: Present version after damage to the past changes its future form.
- Finale: It opens a path deeper into the lab.

This is one of the most developed design sections on the entire site. It shows the creator is thinking cinematically and mechanically at the same time.

Music:
TimeCat has a surprisingly large listed soundtrack preview. The page lists 55 tracks or early track names, including:
- Untitled Title
- Bu ba du ba House Cat
- Bu ba du ba Jungle Cat
- Entrance to the Jungle
- Mysterious Jungle Dance
- Ominous Jungle Dance
- The Lab
- You Ate My Button!
- Lab Boss Norm
- Lab Boss Anger Spikes
- A New Truth
- GlitchFront - The Final Boss
- Unlim8ted Stinger

The number of soundtrack entries suggests either very active musical experimentation or a large amount of concept scoring ahead of full gameplay completion.

Strengths:
TimeCat is the clearest “flagship” project because it has:
- A defined premise
- A central mechanic
- Character dynamics
- Boss concept
- World rules
- Art direction
- UI direction
- Soundtrack direction
- Demo/trailer language

Weaknesses / risks:
The page is extremely polished conceptually, but it is not clear how much of the full design is implemented in a finished game. The public language says “console-quality release,” but the available evidence still points to active development. The project could become very compelling if the actual game matches the design page, but the public proof of completion is limited.

Assessment:
TimeCat is the most marketable Unlim8ted project. It has a strong hook: “a cat eats a time button and flips a lab between past and present.” That is memorable, visual, and game-mechanically useful. If one project should become the studio’s public centerpiece, it is this one.

PROJECT 2: SQUARE PIXEL / SQUARE PIXELS
================================================================================

Category:
Game

Genre:
2D pixelated sandbox adventure

Status:
Public GitHub repository, public website page, substantial commit history, but still visibly unfinished.

Core concept:
Square Pixel is a 2D sandbox adventure inspired by Terraria. It promises exploration, crafting, building, survival, monsters, bosses, multiplayer, modding, procedural generation, and a day/night cycle.

Public positioning:
The Square Pixel product page says:
- Explore, craft, build, and survive in a procedurally generated pixel world.
- Explore diverse biomes, dungeons, and hidden treasures.
- Gather resources and build structures.
- Fight creatures and bosses.
- Multiplayer is planned.
- Modding support is planned.
- Day/night changes affect encounters and strategy.

Technical footprint:
The GitHub repo is one of the most substantial public artifacts connected to Unlim8ted Studios. It has hundreds of commits, visible issues, pull requests, screenshots, and mixed-language code. The listed dependencies include:
- pygame
- pillow
- pymovie
- pyperclip
- playfab
- captcha
- astor

The repository’s language mix includes HTML, Python, C, TeX, C++, and Shell. The GitHub org description says Square Pixels is primarily developed in Python with a multiplayer system in C.

Gameplay:
Controls and objectives are publicly described:
- Move with WASD or arrow keys.
- Open inventory with E.
- Right-click in inventory to create an item.
- Left-click to place an item in a slot.
- Left-click blocks to destroy them.
- Create a character.
- Explore and gather resources.
- Craft items.
- Build structures.
- Defend against monsters and bosses.

Screenshots:
The public page lists screenshots for:
- Night lighting
- Landscape generation
- Inventory
- Procedural generation video
- Character creator

Development state:
The TODO list is extremely important. It includes:
- Fix collision handling
- Add pause screen
- Finish settings
- Add more world options
- Add saving/loading
- Load new chunks
- Add pre-built characters
- Add item drop and inventory integration
- Add crafting and item placing
- Add tools
- Drop items
- Multiplayer
- Friending

This means the game’s public marketing describes the intended full sandbox fantasy, but the public TODO list shows that many fundamental systems are unfinished or incomplete.

Licensing issue:
There appears to be some inconsistency in licensing presentation. The GitHub README displays an MIT badge area, but the actual license section says the project is distributed under a modified Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial 3.0 License. That should be cleaned up because license ambiguity can discourage contributors and users.

Branding issue:
The project appears as both “Square Pixel” and “Square Pixels.” The GitHub repo is “Square-Pixels,” the page headline says “Square Pixel,” and the games page lists “Square Pixels.” This should be standardized.

Strengths:
Square Pixel has the strongest code/project evidence of any game. It is not merely a product concept page. There is a repository, commit history, issue tracking, screenshots, and installation instructions. The idea is familiar but proven: Terraria-inspired sandbox games have an obvious audience.

Weaknesses:
The genre is very ambitious. Building a good Terraria-like game is extremely hard because players expect:
- Stable procedural world generation
- Smooth collision
- Reliable saving/loading
- Huge item systems
- Enemy AI
- Crafting
- Multiplayer
- Biomes
- Progression
- Modding
- Combat balance

Many of these are explicitly unfinished. The project therefore reads as impressive for an independent experiment but not close to a commercial-grade sandbox yet.

Assessment:
Square Pixel is the strongest “developer credibility” project because it has substantial visible work. TimeCat is more marketable; Square Pixel is more technically revealing. If refined, Square Pixel could become the studio’s long-term open sandbox project, but the public page should make its development stage more honest.

The strongest technical footprint is Square Pixel.
The most advanced AI/research concept is Cineme.
The most surprising maker project is Unlim8ted Phone.

The company’s biggest opportunity is curation. There is enough here to look impressive, but the public presentation currently shows too much unfinished material at once.
